# Sales Conversation Guide: Socratic Questions

**Goal:** Guide Young-Wook to conclude *himself* that he needs this service and that it's worth paying well for it.

**Remember:** You're not selling. You're asking questions. Let him do the talking. His answers become his reasons.

---

## How to Use This Guide

1. **Don't read these questions out loud** — internalize them, make them natural
2. **Listen more than you talk** — 70% him, 30% you
3. **Take notes on his answers** — use his exact words later
4. **Never pitch until he's said the pain out loud**
5. **Let silence work** — don't rush to fill gaps

---

# SECTION 1: Guiding Him to "I Need This"

## 1.1 Understanding His Current Situation

Start broad. Let him paint the picture.

| Question | Why You're Asking |
|:---------|:------------------|
| "So tell me — how are things going with sales right now?" | Open-ended. Let him vent or brag. Either reveals truth. |
| "What does your typical week look like when it comes to finding new customers?" | Exposes how manual/painful the process is. |
| "How are you currently reaching out to potential clients?" | Reveals if he has a system or is winging it. |
| "What tools are you using for this?" | Probably nothing or scattered tools. |

---

## 1.2 Surfacing the Pain

Now dig into what's not working. Stay curious, not judgmental.

| Question | Why You're Asking |
|:---------|:------------------|
| "What's the most frustrating part of that process?" | Makes him articulate the pain himself. |
| "How much time do you spend on outreach each week?" | Quantifies the time cost. |
| "And out of all that effort, how many actually convert?" | Exposes poor ROI on his current approach. |
| "What happens to the leads that don't respond the first time?" | Probably nothing. Shows missed opportunity. |

---

## 1.3 Strategic Yes/No Questions (Commitment Ladder)

These get him saying "yes" repeatedly. Builds momentum.

| Question | Expected Answer |
|:---------|:----------------|
| "Would you say lead generation is important for your growth right now?" | Yes |
| "And you'd agree that personalized outreach works better than generic emails?" | Yes |
| "But you don't have time to personally write 100 custom emails, right?" | Right / No |
| "Would it help if that process was mostly automated?" | Yes |
| "So if something could do that for you reliably, that would be valuable?" | Yes |

**He's now said 5 yes's. He's primed.**

---

## 1.4 Future Pacing (Make Him Imagine Success)

| Question | Why You're Asking |
|:---------|:------------------|
| "If you had a system that sent personalized emails to 500 leads a month, what would that mean for your pipeline?" | He imagines the upside. |
| "What would it feel like to wake up and see 10 warm replies in your inbox?" | Emotional connection to the solution. |
| "How would that change what you focus on during the day?" | He sees himself freed up. |

---

## 1.5 Urgency & Cost of Inaction

| Question | Why You're Asking |
|:---------|:------------------|
| "What happens if you don't fix this in the next 3 months?" | Makes him feel the cost of waiting. |
| "How much revenue do you think you're leaving on the table without proper follow-up?" | He quantifies his own loss. |
| "What's the cost of YOU spending 10 hours a week on this instead of product or fundraising?" | Opportunity cost becomes real. |

---

## 1.6 The Transition Question

Once he's said enough pain out loud:

> **"So it sounds like you need a way to reach more leads, personalize at scale, and actually follow up — without it eating all your time. Is that right?"**

If he says **yes**, you've earned the right to present a solution.

---

# SECTION 2: Guiding Him to "It's Worth Paying For"

Now he knows he needs it. Next: he concludes what it's worth.

## 2.1 Anchor to His Revenue

| Question | Why You're Asking |
|:---------|:------------------|
| "What's your average deal size right now?" | Gets a number on the table. |
| "How many new clients would you need this year to hit your target?" | Establishes scale. |
| "So if this system helped you close even 2-3 extra clients, what would that be worth?" | He does the math himself. |

**Example:** If average deal = €10K, and he needs 10 clients → 1 extra client = €10K. The system just paid for itself many times over.

---

## 2.2 Compare to Alternatives

| Question | Why You're Asking |
|:---------|:------------------|
| "Have you looked at hiring someone for sales?" | Probably considered it. Expensive. |
| "What would a full-time SDR cost you in France?" | €40-60K/year. Makes your price look tiny. |
| "What about outsourcing to an agency?" | €2-5K/month. Also expensive. |
| "And those options — do they come with a custom-built system you own?" | No. Yours does. |

---

## 2.3 Value vs. Cost Framing

| Question | Why You're Asking |
|:---------|:------------------|
| "If this system saved you 10 hours a week, what's that worth to you?" | Puts a number on time saved. |
| "What's your hourly rate as a founder?" | Probably €50-100+. 10 hrs = €500-1000/week saved. |
| "So even just on time savings, it pays for itself in the first month?" | Yes. |

---

## 2.4 Make the ROI Obvious

Lead him to calculate it himself:

> **"Let's do quick math together. If the system helps you close just ONE extra deal this quarter, and your average deal is €X... minus the cost of the system... what's your return?"**

Let him say the number out loud. It's always positive.

---

## 2.5 Risk Reversal

| Question | Why You're Asking |
|:---------|:------------------|
| "What would make you feel safe trying this?" | He tells you his objections. |
| "If there was a refund clause if I didn't deliver, would that help?" | Shows you're confident. |
| "Would weekly progress updates make you feel in control?" | Yes. And you already offer this. |

---

## 2.6 The Close (Let Him Say It)

Don't say "so do you want to do this?" Instead:

> **"Based on everything we've talked about — the time you're losing, the deals you're missing, the cost of hiring someone else — does this feel like a good investment?"**

If yes:

> **"Great. What would you need from me to feel ready to move forward?"**

# Summary: The Flow

```
1. Current situation (open questions)
         ↓
2. Pain points (what's not working)
         ↓
3. Yes ladder (build agreement)
         ↓
4. Future vision (imagine success)
         ↓
5. Cost of inaction (urgency)
         ↓
6. Transition ("so you need X, right?")
         ↓
7. Anchor to revenue (his deal size)
         ↓
8. Compare to alternatives (hiring, agencies)
         ↓
9. ROI calculation (he does the math)
         ↓
10. Risk reversal (remove objections)
         ↓
11. Close (let him say yes)
```

---

**Remember:** You're not convincing him. You're helping him convince himself.
