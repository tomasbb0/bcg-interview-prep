## 8) My three decision options

### Option A — Do not sign and leave

* **Advantages for me:** I exit a draining context quickly and free time/headspace to find another startup, a scale‑up/Big Tech role, or structure my own idea.
* **Costs for me:** fear of looking like a quitter; guilt around no revenue; financial/status anxiety without the next step locked.

**48‑hour checklist (A)**

* I write a neutral exit note that thanks the team, states factual contributions, and defines handover.
* I send brief updates to key contacts.
* I lock one pilot for my personal workflow and line up a developer.
* I do a legal consult on confidentiality and any remaining obligations.

**Exit note template (A)**

* “After careful review, I will not proceed with the proposed agreement. I am grateful for the last year, especially the chance to design GTM, run discovery and demos, and structure campaigns and interns. I will be available this week for a clean handover on materials and leads. I wish you both the best building Pairwire.”

### Option B — Sign and stay

* **Advantages for me:** I don’t discard the year, I preserve relationships, I avoid appearing unstable.
* **Costs for me:** it goes against my fairness/clarity criteria; I remain in an ambiguous and weakly protected role; I risk reinforcing my pattern of trying to fix a structure from inside that doesn’t fit me.

**48‑hour checklist (B)**

* I ask for **written acceptance of the equity floor** principle before any other edits.
* I calendar the weekly founder sync.
* I agree on ICP, demo eligibility, and pilot qualification rules that the CTO commits to support.
* I define one 30‑day revenue experiment with clear responsibilities.

**Message to confirm floor principle (B)**

* “Top priority is a time‑vested equity floor that recognizes the unpaid year. Without it everything becomes all or nothing and values this year at zero. If you agree on the floor principle we can close the rest immediately.”

### Option C — Sign the full contract now, then resign after about one month

* **Intent:** obtain formal proof of a founder‑like contract/title, then exit soon after.
* **Advantages for me:** documented founder‑level status for track record; short runway to transition; preserves near‑term optics while I line up next steps.
* **Costs/neutral facts:** a typical 12‑month cliff → likely **zero vested equity** on departure; possible notice periods/hand‑over duties; ongoing confidentiality/IP obligations; potential relationship/reputation friction if exit is very soon; “good/bad leaver” clauses may affect post‑exit rights.

**30‑day checklist (C)**

* I sign, perform courteously, and keep deliverables narrow and demonstrable.
* I prepare handover materials from day one so exit is clean.
* I line up my next team or pilot while honoring time, IP, and confidentiality boundaries.
* I calendar a professional resignation conversation within the month and keep the narrative calm and factual.

**Resignation template (C)**

* “I am grateful for the opportunity and for the formalization period. After the last weeks it is clear that my best contribution sits elsewhere. I will transition respectfully and complete the agreed handover. I wish you continued success with Pairwire.”

## 11) Gemini API Sprint — my playbook

### My framing

* I prototype fast in **AI Studio** in the browser, then translate into **Gemini API** calls for a tiny pilot this week.
* This is **personal R&D**: no Pairwire code, data, or clients; only public material.

### My three demo options

**A) Docs Q&A with citations**

* I upload 3–5 public PDFs (e.g., SOC2, ISO 27001, GDPR DPIA templates, vendor security questionnaires).
* **System prompt:** “You are a due‑diligence analyst. Answer only using the uploaded documents. If not in docs, reply ‘Not in docs’. For every answer append ‘Sources’ plus filename and pages. Be concise, max 6 lines.”
* **User prompts:** “What encryption at rest is declared in these docs” “List data retention commitments and where they appear” “Summarize vendor incident response + sources.”

**B) Lead‑Qualifier Copilot**

* **System prompt:** “You qualify B2B leads for [domain]. Ask ≤6 questions. Then output exactly: 3 pains; one‑line budget/timing; fit score 1–5 with a reason; one‑line next step. Max 6 lines total.”

**C) Meeting‑to‑Proposal Generator**

* **System prompt:** “Turn raw meeting notes into a one‑page proposal with Problem, Approach, Deliverables, Timeline, Price range, Assumptions. Keep it scannable and concrete.”

### My 15‑second opener

“I’m Tomás, GTM founder. I’m validating a Gemini workflow for [due diligence / lead qualification / proposal drafting]. Success today is one developer for a one‑week pilot and one Google startup contact.”

### My 90‑second demo choreography

1. I show the input (docs or notes).
2. I run once.
3. I point to one useful thing in the output.
4. I name the next improvement (JSON→Sheet, simple form, summary email).
5. I make the ask (developer partner + Google contact).

### When asked about my teammate

* “My teammate had a last‑minute conflict, so I’m sprinting solo today. I own GTM and the prototype.”

### Networking lines by audience

* **Googler/organizer:** “I’m validating a Gemini workflow in AI Studio. Who here is from Google for Startups or Cloud Startups so I can get the right program contact”
* **Senior engineer:** “Scope is tiny — Gemini→JSON→Google Sheet→email. Want to pair for a one‑day build and co‑demo next week”
* **Founder/operator:** “If this matches your pain, can I run a mini pilot with you next week”

### Outcomes I want to leave with

* A Google sponsor plus the **exact program name** and a **contact**.
* One developer who commits to a **one‑week pilot**.
* Two warm **intros** to teams with the exact pain for discovery next week.
