# THE NARRATIVE STRATEGY: Controlling Your Story

You need a consistent story that protects your reputation while adapting the tone for different audiences. The core truth is the same: **You were a Co-Founder, you built the foundation, but you exited due to a misalignment on the partnership terms.**

Here is exactly what to say to each group.

---

## 1. To Interviewers & Recruiters (The "Professional Pivot")
**Goal:** Project maturity, competence, and ambition. Avoid bitterness.
**Tone:** Matter-of-fact, forward-looking.

**The Script:**
> "I joined Pairwire as a Co-Founder to build their commercial engine from scratch. It was an intense 0-to-1 experience—I built the sales pipeline, secured pilots with major clients like Enghouse and Mazars, and set up the entire operational infrastructure.
>
> However, when it came time to formalize the long-term partnership agreement, we realized we had different visions for the company’s equity structure and governance. We decided it was best to part ways amicably so I could focus on an opportunity where the incentives are fully aligned. I’m really proud of what we built in those 10 months, and I’m looking for a role where I can apply that same 'builder' mindset."

**Why this works:**
*   **"Equity structure"** is a very common, respectable reason for co-founders to split. It implies you are high-level enough to care about equity, not just a salary.
*   It frames the exit as a **mutual business decision**, not a firing.

---

## 2. To Friends & Peers (The "Smart Move")
**Goal:** Maintain social status, show you are in control, avoid looking like a "victim."
**Tone:** Confident, slightly casual, "insider" vibe.

**The Script:**
> "Yeah, I decided to leave Pairwire. It was a crazy year—we built a lot of cool stuff and got some big clients on board. But honestly, when we sat down to sign the final shareholder deal, the terms just didn't make sense for me anymore.
>
> You know how it is with early-stage startups—if the cap table and the vision aren't 100% aligned, it's better to walk away early than get stuck in a bad marriage. I learned a ton, but I’m relieved to be out and looking for the next thing."

**Why this works:**
*   It sounds like you **chose** to leave because you are smart and know your worth.
*   "Terms didn't make sense" is a powerful phrase that shuts down further questions (people assume it was about money/equity and respect that).

---

## 3. To Family (The "Personal Victory")
**Goal:** Reassure them you are okay, explain why you worked for "free" for so long, and show you stood up for yourself.
**Tone:** Honest, vulnerable but strong.

**The Script:**
> "I know I put a lot of time into this, but I had to draw a line. I worked incredibly hard to build that company, but when the final contract came, they tried to change the deal we had verbally agreed on.
>
> I stood up for myself. I negotiated a clean exit, got them to acknowledge my contributions, and walked away with my head held high. It’s disappointing that they acted that way, but I’m proud that I didn't let them take advantage of me. I have a great track record now, and I’m going to find a place that actually values that."

**Why this works:**
*   It validates their likely concern ("Was this a waste of time?") by turning it into a lesson in self-respect.
*   It frames you as the **hero** of the story, not the victim.

---

## 4. The "Trap" Questions (And how to answer)

**Q: "Did you get fired?"**
*   **A:** "No, we negotiated a mutual exit. It was a strategic disagreement on the partnership terms."

**Q: "Are you suing them?"**
*   **A:** "No, we settled everything amicably. I just want to move forward." (Even if you threatened legal action, you never say you *are* suing unless it's public record. Keep it clean.)

**Q: "What was the problem with the other founder?"**
*   **A:** "Martim is great at product/vision, but we just had different styles when it came to operations and equity. It happens." (Never badmouth him personally. It makes *you* look risky.)
